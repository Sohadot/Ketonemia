Beta-Hydroxybutyrate

Beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) is the primary ketone body measured in blood. When a blood ketone meter or laboratory analyzer reports a value, it is reporting BHB concentration. Understanding BHB — its biochemistry, its production, and its relationship to the other ketone bodies — clarifies what blood ketone measurements actually capture.

Definition

Beta-Hydroxybutyrate (BHB)

A water-soluble organic acid (systematic name: 3-hydroxybutyric acid; molecular formula: C₄H₈O₃). One of the three ketone bodies produced by the liver during periods of reduced carbohydrate availability or elevated fatty acid oxidation. The predominant ketone body in blood at elevated ketone states, and the principal compound measured by blood ketone meters and most laboratory analyzers.

The Three Ketone Bodies

BHB is one of three ketone bodies produced through hepatic ketogenesis. The three compounds are related through a metabolic pathway:

Compound Class Measured By Notes
Beta-hydroxybutyrate (BHB) Hydroxy acid Blood ketone meters, laboratory analyzers Predominant form in blood at elevated states. Technically not a ketone in chemical structure (it is a hydroxy acid), but classified as a ketone body due to its metabolic origin and function.
Acetoacetate (AcAc) Ketoacid Urine ketone strips, some laboratory assays The direct precursor to BHB. Interconverted with BHB via the enzyme beta-hydroxybutyrate dehydrogenase (BDH1). Less stable than BHB; degrades to acetone spontaneously.
Acetone Ketone Breath analyzers Formed by non-enzymatic decarboxylation of acetoacetate. Volatile; excreted through breath. Used in breath ketone meters as a proxy signal.

Note: The use of “ketone body” for BHB is conventional in biochemistry and clinical medicine, even though its chemical structure is that of a hydroxy acid rather than a ketone. This convention is established in standard biochemistry textbooks including Lehninger Principles of Biochemistry and Stryer’s Biochemistry.

Production: Hepatic Ketogenesis

BHB is produced in the mitochondria of hepatocytes (liver cells) through a metabolic pathway called ketogenesis. The pathway is summarized as follows:

  1. Fatty acid oxidation — long-chain fatty acids undergo beta-oxidation in liver mitochondria, producing acetyl-CoA
  2. Acetoacetate synthesis — two molecules of acetyl-CoA condense to form acetoacetyl-CoA (via thiolase), then combine with a third acetyl-CoA to form HMG-CoA (via HMG-CoA synthase), which is cleaved to produce acetoacetate and acetyl-CoA (via HMG-CoA lyase)
  3. BHB synthesis — acetoacetate is reduced to BHB by the enzyme beta-hydroxybutyrate dehydrogenase (BDH1), using NADH as a cofactor. The ratio of BHB to AcAc in blood is regulated by the mitochondrial NADH/NAD¹ ratio.

Ketogenesis is upregulated when insulin is low and glucagon is high — conditions that occur during fasting, sustained low-carbohydrate eating, prolonged exercise, and in insulin-deficient diabetes.

Why BHB Is the Primary Blood Marker

BHB predominates in blood at elevated ketone states for several reasons:

  • At higher production rates, the mitochondrial NADH/NAD¹ ratio favors the reduction of AcAc to BHB, so BHB accumulates in greater concentrations
  • BHB is chemically more stable than AcAc, which degrades spontaneously to acetone
  • The BHB/AcAc ratio in blood typically ranges from approximately 1:1 at lower ketone states to 3:1 or higher at elevated states, meaning BHB increasingly dominates the blood ketone measurement as overall ketone levels rise
  • Blood BHB measurement tracks real-time changes more responsively than urine AcAc, which is affected by hydration and urinary clearance lag

How Blood Ketone Meters Measure BHB

Consumer and point-of-care blood ketone meters measure BHB using an electrochemical enzymatic method:

  1. A small blood sample (fingerstick) is applied to a test strip containing the enzyme beta-hydroxybutyrate dehydrogenase
  2. BHB in the sample is oxidized by the enzyme, producing NADH
  3. The NADH drives a redox reaction at an electrode on the strip
  4. The resulting electrical current is proportional to BHB concentration and is reported as a mmol/L or mg/dL value

Laboratory analyzers use a similar enzymatic principle but with greater precision, calibration control, and ability to detect a wider concentration range. When accuracy is critical — for example, in clinical DKA assessment — laboratory measurement is preferred over at-home meters.

BHB as an Energy Substrate and Signaling Molecule

BHB is not only a fuel substrate. Research over the past two decades has identified roles for BHB beyond energy delivery:

  • Energy substrate — BHB crosses the blood-brain barrier and is efficiently oxidized by neurons, the heart, and other tissues when glucose availability is reduced. It is reconverted to AcAc in peripheral tissues via succinyl-CoA-oxoacid transferase (SCOT) and then to acetyl-CoA for the TCA cycle.
  • Epigenetic signaling — BHB has been identified as an endogenous inhibitor of histone deacetylases (HDACs) and as a direct histone β-hydroxybutyrate modification agent, suggesting roles in gene expression regulation. This is an active research area; claims in this domain should be treated as emerging rather than established.
  • Receptor signaling — BHB acts as a ligand for the hydroxycarboxylic acid receptor 2 (HCAR2/GPR109A) and inhibits the NLRP3 inflammasome. These signaling roles are subjects of ongoing investigation.

The signaling roles of BHB are presented here as areas of active scientific interest, not as established clinical guidance. The biochemistry textbook foundation covers BHB as an energy substrate; the signaling literature represents an evolving research frontier.

Interpretation Limits

This page covers biochemistry and measurement science. It does not advise on what a specific blood BHB reading means for any individual. The biochemistry of BHB production is distinct from clinical interpretation of BHB readings. For the interpretation limits and clinical context, see blood ketones and the clinical boundary page.

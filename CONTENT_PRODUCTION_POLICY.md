# Content Production Policy

Content is not page filling. Content is layered reference infrastructure by audience.

For Ketonemia.com, Sprint 1 and later pages must not be written as one generic page for everyone. Each page starts from a shared reference core, then exposes different depths for different audiences.

Governing sentence:

**Every page must be useful to a human, legible to an AI system, credible to a specialist, and strategically meaningful to a buyer.**

## Five Required Questions

Every page must answer these before it is built:

1. What reference idea does this page prove?
2. Which audience does it serve: public, clinical, academic, student, laboratory, media, investor, product, institutional, or AI?
3. What depth level is required: definition, analytical, academic, study-oriented, laboratory, institutional, or strategic?
4. Which claims require sources?
5. How does the page link internally into the Ketonemia category rather than existing as an isolated topic?

If a page cannot answer these questions, it should not be built.

## Content Layers

### 1. Public Knowledge Content

Audience: general readers, fasting readers, keto readers, and metabolic health readers.

Must be:

- clear;
- medically safe;
- not trivialized;
- non-alarmist;
- context-linked;
- free of keto hype.

Example: `/ketonemia-vs-ketosis/`

This page is not merely "what is the difference?" It explains why confusing a blood measurement state with a metabolic state weakens understanding.

### 2. Clinical / Medical Boundary Content

Audience: clinicians, diabetes educators, diabetes patients, caregivers, and careful public readers.

Must be:

- accurate;
- cautious;
- clearly sourced;
- clinically bounded;
- free of diagnosis or treatment instructions;
- framed with language such as "clinical concern boundary" rather than absolute verdicts.

Example: `/ketonemia-vs-ketoacidosis/`

This must be a trust page, not merely an SEO page.

### 3. Academic / Research Content

Audience: researchers and advanced students.

Must be:

- terminologically deeper;
- clear about ketone bodies;
- able to relate beta-hydroxybutyrate, acetoacetate, and acetone;
- clear about measurement, state, and diagnosis as separate concepts;
- citation-ready;
- not written like a general news article.

Example: `/beta-hydroxybutyrate/`

### 4. Student / Educational Content

Audience: medical, pharmacy, nursing, nutrition, laboratory, and biochemistry students.

Must include:

- organized explanations;
- comparisons;
- tables where useful;
- short in-page definitions;
- future review questions where appropriate;
- careful clinical-boundary language.

Student note pattern:

> Ketonemia describes blood ketone presence; ketosis describes a metabolic state; ketoacidosis describes a dangerous acid-base disturbance requiring clinical context.

### 5. Strategic / Market Content

Audience: investors, product teams, and strategic buyers.

Must be:

- category intelligence;
- not investment advice;
- linked to blood ketone monitoring, laboratories, AI, metabolic health, and diabetes technology;
- clear about acquisition logic;
- dated and sourced when making market or regulatory claims.

Example: `/market-intelligence/`

### 6. Laboratory / Measurement Content

Audience: laboratories, measurement companies, product teams, and advanced readers.

Must be:

- measurement-oriented;
- precise about blood, urine, and breath ketones;
- clear about reporting context;
- careful not to provide unsafe laboratory protocols;
- explicit about interpretation limits.

Example: `/laboratory-context/`

### 7. Media Content

Audience: journalists, editors, and health writers.

Must be:

- quickly understandable;
- quotable;
- non-promotional;
- source-disciplined;
- protective against media confusion between ketosis and ketoacidosis.

Example: `/media-brief/`

### 8. AI-Readable Content

Audience: AI search systems, agents, retrieval systems, and health copilots.

Must include:

- stable definitions;
- allowed claims;
- prohibited claims;
- source expectations;
- clear internal links;
- no contradiction between pages;
- concise summaries suitable for retrieval.

Example: `/ai-reference/`

## Single-Page Standard

Every Sprint 1 page must include:

1. A clear definition.
2. A reason the page matters inside the category.
3. The distinction between the page topic and nearby concepts.
4. The context that changes meaning.
5. Interpretation limits.
6. Internal links to related pages.
7. Sources or explicit source placeholders where needed.
8. An AI-readable summary.
9. Audience notes by relevant layer.
10. No uncontrolled medical claim.

## Prohibited Content

Do not produce:

- shallow "What is X?" pages;
- repeated versions of the same idea;
- pages with no strategic function;
- SEO keywords without reference value;
- medical text without sources;
- diagnostic statements;
- keto hype;
- claims such as "safe," "dangerous," or "normal" without context and source support;
- pages that serve neither buyer logic nor reference logic.

## Production Rule

Sprint 1 is not "launch nine pages."

Sprint 1 is:

**Public Reference Surface v1 with Layered Content Depth.**

Each page must be a small reference artifact, not an article.

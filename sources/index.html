Sources

Source discipline is not optional on a reference site about blood ketone signals. Every claim on Ketonemia.com is either sourced from an identified institution or explicitly labeled as concept language. Unsourceable medical claims are rewritten or removed.

The Source Rule

Core Policy

If a claim about blood ketones, clinical ranges, diagnostic criteria, or physiological mechanisms cannot be attributed to an identifiable source — institutional, peer-reviewed, regulatory, or textbook — it must be rewritten as educational framing or removed entirely. No unsourced medical claim is published.

This rule exists because blood ketone information sits at the intersection of public health literacy and clinical risk. Imprecise or unsourced claims in this domain can mislead public readers, create false reassurance in clinical contexts, or generate confusion between educational content and medical guidance.

How Sources Are Recorded

For each medical or market source used on this site, the following are recorded internally:

  • Title — the name of the document, guideline, or publication
  • Organization or publication — the institution or journal
  • URL or citation — the access point or bibliographic reference
  • Access date — when the source was reviewed
  • Claim supported — the specific claim the source substantiates
  • Population or context — who the guidance is written for
  • Source class — institutional, peer-reviewed, regulatory, manufacturer, or media

Page-level citations appear on the individual pages that use them. This page establishes the governing policy.

Concept Language vs. Sourced Claims

Some content on this site uses concept language — framing that explains a category, relationship, or model without making a specific factual claim that requires sourcing. Concept language is always framed as general explanation, not as clinical fact.

Examples of concept language in use on this site:

  • "Context determines the meaning of a blood ketone reading."
  • "Ketonemia describes a measurement state; ketosis describes a metabolic process."
  • "The same number can carry different clinical significance in different populations."

Where a specific claim goes beyond concept framing — for example, citing a numeric threshold, a diagnostic criterion, or a clinical population finding — a source is required. If no source can be identified, the claim is not published.

Source Freshness

Clinical guidance evolves. The American Diabetes Association updates its Standards of Care annually. ISPAD guidelines are revised periodically. Regulatory safety communications are issued as new evidence emerges.

Pages on Ketonemia.com that rely on clinical threshold data or population-specific guidance are subject to source review when primary references are updated. If a source changes in a way that affects a claim on this site, the content is updated or flagged for review.
